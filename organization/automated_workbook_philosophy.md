We measure audience performance by:

- **How** much traffic we generate
- **When** we had the traffic
- **Where** the traffic came from
- **What** created the traffic

We use these signals to determine **Why** events played out the way they did, so we can **take action** to consistently maintain and grow performance.

## Traffic Measurement Bands
Regardless of platform, we typically organize traffic measurement in the following ways:

| bucket | example |
| :---: | :---: |
| Brand | GQ, Wired, Vogue, etc. |
| Timeframe | Daily, Weekly, Monthly, Quarterly, or Annually |
| Location | United States, Western Europe, Domestic, Multi-market |
| Source | Google Search, Related Videos, etc. |
| Content | *most savored recipes*, *Christian Bale Breaks Down His Most Iconic Characters* |
| Collection | *Open Door*, *Iconic Characters*, *Expert Advice*, *Books* |

The key is ensuring that all **developer** tools pull from a **data governed** source of truth, which ensures accurate measurement.

## Audience Workbook consolidation
Audience Workbooks are automated ecosystems, which act as lightweight sandboxes for analytical exploration. Overtime, they found a footing in the organization because of their accessibility, ease of use, and natural compatability with other commonly used productivity tools (such as Slides, Docs, and Gmail).

This also results in an informal relationship where the workbooks continue existing without a sunsetting or promotion process.

It now seems appropriate to unify the syntax and structure used to measure performance accross platforms where Condé Nast content appears, such that new collections of workbooks exist with a familiar structure regardless of the format of the content.

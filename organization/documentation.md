# how to document things
writing things down is important. it seems to be the part of the project that people often overlook and/or do not look forward to.

i *highly* encourage you to develop the habit. everyone will benefit.

you will forget aspects of the project as you get further away from doing the things you did. additionally, you can allow others to take over the project if you leave a written record that allows them to understand why it exists; how it works; and what it's purpose is.

with that in mind, i encourage a straightforward method to docmentation.

## orgaizing principle
all of my documents have the following:

1. my name
2. my title
3. the date
4. **what** the document is about
5. **who** is asking for it/**who** is doing something
6. **what** it is for/**what** it depends on
7. **when** it is updated/**when** is was created
8. **where** it runs/**where** it is sourced from
9. **why** it is happening/**why** it works the way it does
10. **how** it is maintained/**how** it works

## not all documentation is the same
the organizing principles act as a guide; not a commandment. each project benefits from it's own context. the who-what-when-where-why-how system provides a framework to anticipate helpful things for you and your future collaborators/users to work independently of one another. 

## screenshots
whenever you write a document you should aspire to never have to type the words again. meaning, you can either take a screenshot of a passage **or** you can link to the document and answer someone's question. 

this will save you time and empower the other person.

## linking strategy
instead of writing really long documents, consider creating highly insightful passages that can be linked in other documents. this cuts down on the amount of writing you have to do at one time and increases the value of the writing.

## don't bore us, get to the chorus
if you write the greatest document in the history of the world, but it's in a lnaguage no one can read, then it isn't the greatest document in the world. write effectively, which means more than word choice: think about your audience (and their attention span).
